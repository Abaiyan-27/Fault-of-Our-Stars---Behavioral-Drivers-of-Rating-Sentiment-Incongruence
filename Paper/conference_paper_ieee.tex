\title{Understanding Structural and Behavioural Drivers of Sentiment--Rating Incongruence in Sri Lankan Tourism Reviews}

\author{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Star ratings are widely used as ground-truth sentiment labels in opinion mining, yet their alignment with review text remains under-examined. Sentiment--rating incongruence refers to a mismatch between the star rating given by a reviewer and the sentiment expressed in the review text. This study investigates this phenomenon using tourism attraction reviews from Sri Lanka. A transformer-based sentiment pipeline identifies incongruence in 18.6\% of reviews, structured across six directional patterns, with Conservative Rater and Obligatory 5-Star behaviours accounting for 66.7\% of all mismatches. Incongruence varies significantly across venue types, with museums exhibiting the highest rates. A multi-stage analytical framework, combining statistical tests, logit modeling, Random Forest, and SHAP, reveals venue type, reviewer expertise, and review length as key predictors. Predictive performance remains modest (AUC $\approx 0.58$--$0.61$), indicating that incongruence is structured yet only partially explainable using observable features. These findings challenge the assumption, ratings reliably represent textual sentiment and highlight the presence of systematic label distortion in review data, with implications for sentiment modeling and tourism platform design.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Sentiment Analysis, Sentiment-Rating Incongruence, Tourism Reviews, Sri Lanka, Logistic Regression, SHAP
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
If someone is planning to go on a tour to a place, one of the main sources that they look first are reviews by the previous travellers. Usually almost all tourism platforms present both star ratings and the written text sentiments for others to get an understanding about the destinations. In the scope of sentiment analysis, these star ratings are taken as the reference values/labels, which provide an easy mapping to sentiment polarity \cite{ref1,ref2}. But, this assumption hasn’t really been tested much even though there are instances where the star ratings and the text sentiment does not match at all, even introducing noise to the downstream models. The rapid growth of user-generated content on sites like TripAdvisor has greatly increased the amount of review data available \cite{ref9}. However, the reliability of numerical ratings with respect to the text sentiment have not been analyzed at an equivalent pace.

These divergences in the ratings and texts are actually not errors, but it reflects on how a consistent reviewers’ behaviour is driven by their expectations, experience, and the context. For example, one visitor might write "genuinely fascinating" about a museum but still gives three stars because he expected more, while on the other hand, another might rate a temple five stars out of cultural reverence and might write "it was okay, quite crowded". So, this means the numbers and the words are telling two different stories. In this study, we define this as “sentiment-rating incongruence” and will treat it as a structured behavioral pattern rather than just a problem of how responses get labeled. Many large scale review datasets from previous dataset analysis have proved that a considerable amount of reviews display some form of inconsistency between the sentiment polarity and the given star rating score \cite{ref5,ref6}, demonstrating the experimental instability of the assumption of rating-text alignment. Deep learning methods, particularly transformer-based models, have exhibited greater potential to identify these tiny distinctions when compared to traditional lexicon-based approaches \cite{ref10,ref11}.

Despite its importance, incongruence remains insufficiently studied. Previous research predominantly uses ratings as proxy labels, concentrating mainly on hotels and restaurants in closely studied areas \cite{ref2,ref12}. Furthermore, topic modelling and aspect-based sentiment analysis have improved our understanding of the relationship between specific service aspects and overall ratings \cite{ref13,ref14}. However, the fundamental issue of systematic incongruence across review types and venue categories has not been properly investigated. Research conducted in Sri Lanka identifies incompatibility between ratings and review texts in hotel reviews \cite{ref3,ref4}. Additionally, broader studies highlight contextual variation and systematic inconsistency \cite{ref5,ref6,ref7}.

Despite growing interest in online reviews, the understanding is relatively little about how common sentiment-rating incongruence is, what forms it takes, and what causes it, specially in tourism context. Reviews of hospitality research confirm that attractions, particularly in emerging destinations like Sri Lanka, have received lower priority than hotels and restaurants. At the same time, advances in zero-shot and transfer learning mean it is now possible to study this phenomenon at a larger scale without needing manually labelled data. This study draws on a dataset on popular Sri Lankan a	raction reviews spanning 2010 to 2023 to examine how sentiment is expressed differently across text and star ratings.

\section{Related Work}
The foundational survey by Pang and Lee \cite{ref1} has established the sentiment analysis as a traditional area, with the assumption that star ratings actually reflect on the sentiment which was written in text. Even with the recognized imperfections in this assumption, that convention still remains in the tourism research area as a practical labelling strategy and as a reference benchmarking level. Alaei et al. \cite{ref2} have observed that the star ratings are widely considered as weak labels without a clear validation, with their research being heavily concentrated on hotels and restaurants data. This imbalance is further confirmed by Ameur et al. \cite{ref12}, where they highlighted the limited venue diversity and geographic coverage in existing studies, especially for emerging tourist destinations.

Methodological advances have significantly increased the capabilities of sentiment analysis. Wen et al. \cite{ref10} demonstrated the effectiveness of the transformer-based models like Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformer (BERT) \cite{ref16} and Enhanced Representation through Knowledge Integration (ERNIE) \cite{ref17}, while Puh and Bagic Babac \cite{ref11} has shown that predicting both sentiment and the ratings has given more deeper understanding. A multilingual approach along with an aspect based approach will make interpretation much easier \cite{ref13,ref14}. Moreover, some recent zeroshot approaches help to analyze uncommon data without providing any additional information \cite{ref15}.

In the context of Sri Lanka, Abeysinghe and Bandara \cite{ref3,ref4} documented rating-text incompatibility in hotel reviews, but they rely on lexicon-based methods and treat inconsistency primarily as a labeling issue. On the other hand, this study extends the scope to tourism attraction reviews, while making use of transformer-based models, and formulates incongruence as a behavioural aspect requiring context rather than correcting them, and with that, shifting the analytical perspective.

Studies relating to the relationship between ratings and the text are inconclusive. According to Bigne et al. \cite{ref5}, there is a general alignment with some variations based on context, but in contrast, George and Ramos \cite{ref9} have discovered that ratings tend to be greater than sentiment in most destination related reviews. Kwon et al. \cite{ref6} further shows that the inconsistency varies with the context and impacts the helpfulness of the reviews.

Reviewer characteristics also play a critical role. Chua and Banerjee \cite{ref7} show that reviewer expertise affects the relationship between ratings and textual content, whereas the previous work connects sentiment polarity and review depth to perceived usefulness \cite{ref7,ref12}, suggesting that ratings and text capture different aspects of the user experience.

In general, however, the problem of sentiment-rating inconsistency is incompletely understood, especially in emerging tourist destinations. While recent advancements in transformer-based and aspect-based methods facilitate large-scale, precise analysis \cite{ref10,ref11,ref13,ref14} and minimize labeling needs \cite{ref15}, to the best of our knowledge, no existing research attempts to investigate the nature of directionally inconsistent patterns and their fundamental factors using a multi-venue, longitudinal dataset. So, this study tries to address this gap using a large-scale Sri Lankan tourism review dataset spanning diverse attraction types and extended time frame \cite{ref8}, providing new experimental results from an underrepresented context.

\section{Methodology}

measuring, structuring, and explaining sentiment--rating incongruence in tourism
reviews. Rather than treating rating--text mismatch as residual noise, the
framework operationalizes it as a behavioural outcome, links it to contextual
and reviewer-level predictors, and evaluates it through complementary

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

The framework begins by converting a large, venue-diverse review collection into
an analytical dataset suitable for studying mismatch across space, time, and
reviewer behaviour. The study used the ``Tourism and Travel Reviews: Sri Lankan
Destinations'' dataset from Mendeley Data~\cite{ref8}, which contains 16,156
reviews from 2010 to 2023 across 11 attraction types in Sri Lanka. Missing
values and duplicates were checked during preprocessing. Date fields were used
to create travel year and review delay, with negative delay values set to zero.
Raw location text was processed using rule-based parsing and manual mapping to
identify province and district. \textit{Review\_Length} was calculated as the
character count of the review text. Star ratings were grouped into three
classes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Negative (1--2$\star$)
    \item Neutral (3$\star$)
    \item Positive (4--5$\star$)
\end{itemize}
This grouping follows common practice in sentiment
analysis~\cite{ref1,ref2} and makes the rating scale directly comparable with
the three-class sentiment output. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_attributes} presents
the source columns used to build the analytical dataset.

the pretrained cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment model performed best
overall and was selected to label the full dataset. This approach allowed
sentiment to be identified from the review text rather than assumed from the

\subsection{Variable Construction}

After sentiment labelling, raw title and text were removed from further
analysis. \textit{Incongruent} was defined as a mismatch between
\textit{Sentiment} and \textit{Rating\_Class}, while \textit{Pattern} recorded
the six mismatch types. \textit{Reviewer\_Tier} grouped reviewers as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Novice (0--5)
    \item Casual (6--20)
    \item Active (21--100)
    \item Expert (101+)
\end{itemize}
Additional derived variables included \textit{log\_review\_length},
\textit{log\_review\_delay}, \textit{Travel\_Year\_c}, and
\textit{Travel\_Year\_c2}. Table~\ref{tab:derived_variables} presents the

\subsection{Statistical Testing and Predictive Modelling}

The final stage analysed predictors of sentiment--rating incongruence using
both statistical tests and predictive models. \textit{Incongruent} was used as
the binary outcome variable, while variables used to define it were excluded
from the predictors to avoid data leakage. The final predictor set included
venue type, province, reviewer tier, review length, review delay, and travel
year terms, with low multicollinearity (max VIF~$= 3.663$).

Chi-square and Mann--Whitney U tests were used for bivariate analysis, with
Benjamini--Hochberg correction for multiple testing. Logistic regression and
logit models were used to examine linear effects and adjusted odds ratios,
while Random Forest and SHAP were used to identify nonlinear effects and
feature importance. Model performance was evaluated using AUC-ROC.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Prevalence and Six-Pattern Typology}
Among the 16,156 tourism attraction reviews analysed, 3,005 were identified as
incongruent, giving an overall prevalence of 18.6\%, or roughly one in five
reviews. Fig.~\ref{fig:incongruence_patterns} further shows that incongruence follows a
six-pattern
typology. The two most common patterns, Conservative Rater (38.4\%) and
Obligatory 5-Star (28.3\%), together account for 66.7\% of all incongruent
reviews. This indicates that rating--text mismatch is directionally structured
rather than random. In addition, Frustrated Neutral and Polite Inflator account
for a further 24.2\% of incongruent cases, showing that negative sentiment is
often paired with non-negative ratings. These findings suggest that
incongruence reflects consistent behavioural patterns, supporting further
analysis across reviewer and contextual factors.

\subsection{Variation Across Venue Types}
Incongruence rates were compared across 11 attraction categories to assess
contextual variation. The Chi-square test showed a statistically significant
association between venue type and incongruence, with a small but meaningful
effect size ($\chi^2(10) = 125.85$, $p < 0.001$; Cramer's V = 0.088). As shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:venue_incongruence_rate}, National Parks had the lowest
incongruence rate (12.8\%), whereas Museums had the highest (26.3\%). Relative
to National Parks, museum reviews
were 2.43 times more likely to be incongruent. Overall, the variation across
venue types indicates that rating--text mismatch is not evenly distributed, but
differs by review context.

\subsection{Predictors of Incongruence}
Bivariate screening with Benjamini--Hochberg correction was used to identify
predictors associated with incongruence. As shown in
Table~\ref{tab:bivariate_screening}, reviewer tier, province, travel year, and
review length remained significant after correction,
while review delay was not significant ($q = 0.7503$). Expert reviewers were
1.97 times more likely than novices to produce incongruent reviews. In
addition, incongruent reviews were longer at the median than congruent reviews
(296 vs. 279 characters). These findings indicate that both reviewer
characteristics and review content are associated with sentiment--rating
mismatch, although multivariable modelling is needed to test their independent
effects.

\subsection{Model-Based Analysis and Interpretation}
\text{1) Model 1A -- Logistic Regression (Baseline Discrimination):}
A class-balanced logistic regression model was used as a linear baseline. It
achieved a mean cross-validated AUC of $0.5890 \pm 0.0093$ and a test AUC of
0.5840, indicating modest but stable predictive performance. This suggests that
incongruence is only partly explained by the observed variables.

\text{2) Model 1B -- Explanatory Logit (Primary Inference Model):}
The explanatory logit model was used to identify independent predictors of
incongruence. Based on 95\% confidence intervals, 19 predictors were
statistically significant. Venue type, reviewer expertise, review length, and
travel year showed important effects, while review delay had only a weak
negative association. Overall, the model shows that incongruence is shaped by
structural, behavioural, and temporal factors.

\text{3) Model 2 -- Random Forest (Nonlinear Structure Assessment):}
A Random Forest model was applied to capture nonlinear relationships and
interactions. It achieved a test AUC of 0.6095, which was slightly higher than
logistic regression. This indicates that nonlinear effects are present, although
their contribution is modest.

\text{4) SHAP Analysis -- Interpreting Nonlinear Behaviour:}
SHAP analysis was used to interpret the Random Forest model. The most
influential predictors were review length, expert tier, travel year, review
delay, and museums. The SHAP results were broadly consistent with the logit
model, while also showing that some effects vary across contexts. Together,
these findings suggest that incongruence is structured but only partly
explained by the available predictors.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Structured Incongruence and Conservative Rating Behaviour }
An incongruence rate of 18.6\% (approximately 1 in 5 reviews) indicates that
rating--text mismatch is a systematic phenomenon rather than random noise. This
pattern appears to reflect context-dependent evaluation behaviour. For example,
religious sites may attract high ratings despite mixed sentiment, while museums
may involve multidimensional experiences that are not easily captured by a
single score. This interpretation is reinforced by the dominance of the
Conservative Rater pattern, which combines positive text with moderate ratings
and accounts for 38.4\% of all incongruent reviews. Its prevalence increases
from 27.4\% among novice reviewers to 40.4\% among experts, supporting the view
that more experienced reviewers apply stricter or more calibrated rating
standards. Taken together, these findings suggest that star ratings do not
always provide reliable ground-truth labels for textual sentiment and may
introduce systematic bias into sentiment models, particularly when positive
experiences are expressed through neutral ratings.


\subsection{Location Type as a Structural Moderator of Label Reliability}
Location type emerges as an important determinant of sentiment--rating
incongruence. Compared with National Parks, museum reviews show substantially
higher odds of mismatch (Adjusted OR = 2.386), with similar patterns observed
for beaches, bodies of water, zoological gardens, and waterfalls. One possible
explanation is that some attraction types involve more complex and
multidimensional experiences, making them harder to summarise with a single
numerical rating. In other cases, such as religious sites, social or cultural
expectations may shape how ratings are assigned despite mixed textual
sentiment. These results suggest that the reliability of star ratings varies
across review contexts. As a result, treating all reviews as equally reliable
labels may introduce systematic bias, highlighting the need for more
context-aware sentiment modelling.

\subsection{Complementary Roles of Linear and Nonlinear Models}
The results indicate that linear and nonlinear models provide complementary
perspectives on sentiment--rating incongruence rather than competing
explanations. The logistic regression baseline achieved a test AUC of 0.584,
providing a stable reference for linear predictive performance, while the
explanatory logit model identified 19 statistically significant predictors and
quantified their independent effects through adjusted odds ratios. The Random
Forest model achieved a slightly higher test AUC of 0.6095, confirming the
presence of nonlinear relationships and interaction effects. However, the
modest improvement suggests that these nonlinearities are present but not
dominant. Importantly, the overall predictive range (AUC $\approx 0.58$--$0.61$)
should not be interpreted only as a modelling limitation. Instead, it suggests
that incongruence is a structured but only partially observable phenomenon,
with substantial variation likely driven by latent behavioural and contextual
factors. SHAP analysis reinforces this interpretation by showing that
predictors such as reviewer expertise, review length, and venue type
contribute in nonlinear and context-dependent ways. Taken together, these
findings show that incongruence is not random, but its full explanation
requires richer behavioural and contextual information than is available in the
current feature set.

\subsection{Review Delay and Reviewer Expertise Effects }
Review delay appears to play only a limited role in explaining sentiment--rating
incongruence. It was not significant in the bivariate analysis and showed only
a weak negative association in the logit model (OR = 0.960). Although SHAP
ranked it as relatively influential, its effect remained modest and
context-dependent, suggesting that it is a secondary factor rather than a
primary driver. In contrast, reviewer expertise shows a much clearer
relationship with mismatch. The prevalence of the Conservative Rater pattern
rises from 27.4\% among novice reviewers to 40.4\% among experts, while more
extreme patterns such as Harsh Deflator decline from 10.8\% to 4.2\%. This
pattern suggests that experienced reviewers are more calibrated in how they use
ratings, and that incongruence is influenced more by reviewer behaviour than by
the timing of review submission. These findings highlight the importance of
reviewer-level characteristics in understanding rating--text mismatch.

 


\section{Conclusion}
This study shows that sentiment--rating incongruence in tourism reviews is not
random noise, but a structured phenomenon shaped by reviewer behaviour and
contextual evaluation complexity. By combining statistical inference,
predictive modelling, and interpretable machine learning, the analysis links
mismatch to factors such as reviewer calibration, multidimensional evaluation,
and context-specific rating norms.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The study is based on a single Sri
Lankan dataset, which may limit generalisability to other tourism settings. It
also relies on structured features and model-derived sentiment labels, which
may introduce measurement error and may not capture deeper linguistic nuance.
In addition, the modest predictive performance (AUC $\approx$ 0.58--0.61)
suggests that a substantial share of variation remains unexplained, likely due
to latent behavioural and contextual factors that were not directly observed.

Despite these limitations, the findings highlight an important implication for
both NLP and platform design: star ratings may not always function as reliable
ground-truth labels for textual sentiment. Instead, rating--text inconsistency
should be understood as a meaningful behavioural signal rather than simple
noise. Future research should test this phenomenon across other contexts and
incorporate richer semantic and behavioural features to improve explanatory
power.
