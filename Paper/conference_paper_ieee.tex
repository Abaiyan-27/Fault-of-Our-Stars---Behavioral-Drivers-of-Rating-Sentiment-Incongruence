\title{Understanding Structural and Behavioural Drivers of Sentiment--Rating Incongruence in Sri Lanka Tourism Reviews}

\author{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Star ratings are widely used as ground-truth sentiment labels in opinion mining, yet their alignment with review text remains under-examined. Sentiment--rating incongruence refers to a mismatch between the star rating given by a reviewer and the sentiment expressed in the review text. This study investigates this phenomenon using tourism attraction reviews from Sri Lanka. A transformer-based sentiment pipeline identifies incongruence in 18.6\% of reviews, structured across six directional patterns, with Conservative Rater and Obligatory 5-Star behaviours accounting for 66.7\% of all mismatches. Incongruence varies significantly across venue types, with museums exhibiting the highest rates. A multi-stage analytical framework, combining statistical tests, logit modeling, Random Forest, and SHAP, reveals venue type, reviewer expertise, and review length as key predictors. Predictive performance remains modest (AUC $\approx 0.58$--$0.61$), indicating that incongruence is structured yet only partially explainable using observable features. These findings challenge the assumption, ratings reliably represent textual sentiment and highlight the presence of systematic label distortion in review data, with implications for sentiment modeling and tourism platform design.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Sentiment Analysis, Sentiment-Rating Incongruence, Tourism Reviews, Sri Lanka, Logistic Regression, SHAP

\section{Introduction}
If someone is planning to go on a tour to a place, one of the main sources that they look first are reviews by the previous travellers. Usually almost all tourism platforms present both star ratings and the written text sentiments for others to get an understanding about the destinations. In the scope of sentiment analysis, these star ratings are taken as the reference values/labels, which provide an easy mapping to sentiment polarity \cite{ref1,ref2}. But, this assumption hasn’t really been tested much even though there are instances where the star ratings and the text sentiment does not match at all, even introducing noise to the downstream models. The rapid growth of user-generated content on sites like TripAdvisor has greatly increased the amount of review data available \cite{ref9}. However, the reliability of numerical ratings with respect to the text sentiment have not been analyzed at an equivalent pace.

These divergences in the ratings and texts are actually not errors, but it reflects on how a consistent reviewers’ behaviour is driven by their expectations, experience, and the context. For example, one visitor might write "genuinely fascinating" about a museum but still gives three stars because he expected more, while on the other hand, another might rate a temple five stars out of cultural reverence and might write "it was okay, quite crowded". So, this means the numbers and the words are telling two different stories. In this study, we define this as “sentiment-rating incongruence” and will treat it as a structured behavioral pattern rather than just a problem of how responses get labeled. Many large scale review datasets from previous dataset analysis have proved that a considerable amount of reviews display some form of inconsistency between the sentiment polarity and the given star rating score \cite{ref5,ref6}, demonstrating the experimental instability of the assumption of rating-text alignment. Deep learning methods, particularly transformer-based models, have exhibited greater potential to identify these tiny distinctions when compared to traditional lexicon-based approaches \cite{ref10,ref11}.

Despite its importance, incongruence remains insufficiently studied. Previous research predominantly uses ratings as proxy labels, concentrating mainly on hotels and restaurants in closely studied areas \cite{ref2,ref12}. Furthermore, topic modelling and aspect-based sentiment analysis have improved our understanding of the relationship between specific service aspects and overall ratings \cite{ref13,ref14}. However, the fundamental issue of systematic incongruence across review types and venue categories has not been properly investigated. Research conducted in Sri Lanka identifies incompatibility between ratings and review texts in hotel reviews \cite{ref3,ref4}. Additionally, broader studies highlight contextual variation and systematic inconsistency \cite{ref5,ref6,ref7}.

Despite growing interest in online reviews, the understanding is relatively little about how common sentiment-rating incongruence is, what forms it takes, and what causes it, specially in tourism context. Reviews of hospitality research confirm that attractions, particularly in emerging destinations like Sri Lanka, have received lower priority than hotels and restaurants. At the same time, advances in zero-shot and transfer learning mean it is now possible to study this phenomenon at a larger scale without needing manually labelled data. This study draws on a dataset on popular Sri Lankan a	raction reviews spanning 2010 to 2023 to examine how sentiment is expressed differently across text and star ratings.

\section{Related Work}
The foundational survey by Pang and Lee \cite{ref1} has established the sentiment analysis as a traditional area, with the assumption that star ratings actually reflect on the sentiment which was written in text. Even with the recognized imperfections in this assumption, that convention still remains in the tourism research area as a practical labelling strategy and as a reference benchmarking level. Alaei et al. \cite{ref2} have observed that the star ratings are widely considered as weak labels without a clear validation, with their research being heavily concentrated on hotels and restaurants data. This imbalance is further confirmed by Ameur et al. \cite{ref12}, where they highlighted the limited venue diversity and geographic coverage in existing studies, especially for emerging tourist destinations.

Methodological advances have significantly increased the capabilities of sentiment analysis. Wen et al. \cite{ref10} demonstrated the effectiveness of the transformer-based models like Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformer (BERT) \cite{ref16} and Enhanced Representation through Knowledge Integration (ERNIE) \cite{ref17}, while Puh and Bagic Babac \cite{ref11} has shown that predicting both sentiment and the ratings has given more deeper understanding. A multilingual approach along with an aspect based approach will make interpretation much easier \cite{ref13,ref14}. Moreover, some recent zeroshot approaches help to analyze uncommon data without providing any additional information \cite{ref15}.

In the context of Sri Lanka, Abeysinghe and Bandara \cite{ref3,ref4} documented rating-text incompatibility in hotel reviews, but they rely on lexicon-based methods and treat inconsistency primarily as a labeling issue. On the other hand, this study extends the scope to tourism attraction reviews, while making use of transformer-based models, and formulates incongruence as a behavioural aspect requiring context rather than correcting them, and with that, shifting the analytical perspective.

Studies relating to the relationship between ratings and the text are inconclusive. According to Bigne et al. \cite{ref5}, there is a general alignment with some variations based on context, but in contrast, George and Ramos \cite{ref9} have discovered that ratings tend to be greater than sentiment in most destination related reviews. Kwon et al. \cite{ref6} further shows that the inconsistency varies with the context and impacts the helpfulness of the reviews.

Reviewer characteristics also play a critical role. Chua and Banerjee \cite{ref7} show that reviewer expertise affects the relationship between ratings and textual content, whereas the previous work connects sentiment polarity and review depth to perceived usefulness \cite{ref7,ref12}, suggesting that ratings and text capture different aspects of the user experience.

In general, however, the problem of sentiment-rating inconsistency is incompletely understood, especially in emerging tourist destinations. While recent advancements in transformer-based and aspect-based methods facilitate large-scale, precise analysis \cite{ref10,ref11,ref13,ref14} and minimize labeling needs \cite{ref15}, to the best of our knowledge, no existing research attempts to investigate the nature of directionally inconsistent patterns and their fundamental factors using a multi-venue, longitudinal dataset. So, this study tries to address this gap using a large-scale Sri Lankan tourism review dataset spanning diverse attraction types and extended time frame \cite{ref8}, providing new experimental results from an underrepresented context.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

This study used the ``Tourism and Travel Reviews: Sri Lankan Destinations''
dataset from Mendeley Data~\cite{ref8}. The dataset contains 16,156 reviews from
2010 to 2023 across 11 attraction types in Sri Lanka. Preprocessing checked
missing values and duplicate records. Date fields were parsed to derive travel
year and review delay, and negative delays were clipped at zero. Rule-based
parsing and manual mappings were used to identify province and district.
\textit{Review\_Length} was defined as the character count of the review text.
\textit{Rating\_Class} grouped star ratings as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Negative (1--2$\star$)
    \item Neutral (3$\star$)
    \item Positive (4--5$\star$)
\end{itemize}
This grouping follows common practice~\cite{ref1,ref2}.

\subsection{Statistical Testing and Predictive Modelling}

Location name, city, user country, user locale, raw date fields, and title
length were excluded from the modelling matrix. The final explanatory design
matrix contained 24 predictors: 10 venue-type dummies, 7 province dummies, 3
reviewer-tier dummies, and 4 continuous terms
(\textit{log\_review\_length}, \textit{log\_review\_delay},
\textit{Travel\_Year\_c}, and \textit{Travel\_Year\_c}$^2$). Reference groups
were National Parks, Southern Province, and Novice reviewers. Multicollinearity
was low (max VIF~$= 3.663$).

Bivariate screening used chi-square tests for categorical predictors and the
Mann--Whitney U test for continuous predictors. Cram\'{e}r's V was reported for
venue-type comparisons. Benjamini--Hochberg correction was applied only to the
five main screening tests ($q < 0.05$).

Model~1A was a class-balanced logistic regression. It used a stratified 80\%
training and 20\% test split, 5-fold stratified cross-validation on the training
set, and AUC-ROC on the held-out test set. Model~1B was an unweighted
\texttt{statsmodels} logit fit on the full design matrix to estimate adjusted
odds ratios and 95\% confidence intervals. Model~2 was a Random Forest tuned
with \texttt{GridSearchCV} on the training set and evaluated on the same test
set. SHAP values were computed with TreeExplainer on the best Random Forest.
AUC-ROC was the main performance measure because the outcome was imbalanced.

These structured patterns motivate further analysis across contextual and reviewer-related factors. 

\subsection{Variation Across Venue Types}
To examine contextual variation, incongruence rates were compared across 11 attraction categories. A chi-square test confirms a statistically significant association between venue type and incongruence ($\chi^2(10) = 125.85$, $p < 0.001$), with a small but meaningful effect size (Cramer's V = 0.088).

A clear gradient is observed (Table~\ref{tab:venue_type_rates}), with National Parks exhibiting the lowest incongruence rate (12.8\%) and Museums the highest (26.3\%). This corresponds to an odds ratio of 2.43, indicating that museum reviews are more than twice as likely to be incongruent relative to the baseline. The 13.5 percentage-point spread across categories is both substantial and systematically structured, suggesting that mismatch varies meaningfully with contextual characteristics of the experience.

\subsection{Model-Based Analysis and Interpretation}
\subsubsection{Model 1A -- Logistic Regression (Baseline Discrimination)}
A class-balanced logistic regression model with five-fold stratified cross-validation achieves a mean CV AUC of  $0.5890 \pm 0.0093$  and a test AUC of 0.5840, indicating modest but stable performance. This baseline captures the linear predictive signal, while the low AUC suggests that incongruence is influenced by latent behavioural factors beyond observable features. 


\subsubsection{Model 1B -- Explanatory Logit (Primary Inference Model)}
A statsmodels logit model was fitted to identify independent predictors of incongruence, with adjusted odds ratios accounting for all other variables. Statistical significance was assessed using 95\% confidence intervals (CI), where predictors are significant if the CI excludes 1. A total of 19 predictors meet this criterion, indicating strong systematic relationships. 

Venue type emerges as a key determinant. Relative to National Parks, Museums (OR = 2.386), Beaches (2.171), Bodies of Water (2.022), Religious Sites (1.492), Zoological Gardens (1.725), Waterfalls (1.324), and Nature \& Wildlife Areas (1.279) show significantly higher odds of incongruence (CI $>$ 1). In contrast, Gardens (1.265), Farms (1.224), and Historic Sites (1.163) are not statistically significant (CI includes 1), indicating no clear effect.

Reviewer expertise exhibits a clear gradient, with Expert (1.740), Active (1.522), and Casual (1.243) reviewers all significantly more likely to produce incongruence compared to Novices, consistent with a calibration-based interpretation of rating behaviour.

Geographic effects are also evident. Several provinces show elevated odds, including Northern (1.787), Sabaragamuwa (1.641), North Central (1.601), Central (1.586), Uva (1.523), and Western (1.382), while Eastern Province (1.066) is not significant.

Review characteristics indicate that review length (OR = 1.219) has a strong positive association with incongruence, whereas review delay (OR = 0.960) shows a weak but statistically significant negative effect, suggesting only a marginal influence.

Temporal effects reveal a significant negative association for Travel\_Year\_c (OR = 0.946, CI $<$ 1), indicating that incongruence decreases over time. The quadratic term (Travel\_Year\_c$^2$: OR = 0.995) is not statistically significant, providing no evidence of nonlinear temporal effects and suggesting an approximately linear trend.

Overall, the model provides the study's primary explanatory insight by quantifying the independent and directional effects of structural, behavioural, temporal, and contextual predictors on the likelihood of incongruence.

\subsubsection{Model 2 -- Random Forest (Nonlinear Structure Assessment)}
To capture nonlinear relationships and interactions, a Random Forest model was trained on the same dataset. It achieves a test AUC of 0.6095, improving by +0.0256 over logistic regression, confirming the presence of nonlinear effects. However, the modest gain indicates these effects are not dominant, positioning the Random Forest as a complementary diagnostic model rather than a replacement for the logit.

\subsubsection{SHAP Analysis -- Interpreting Nonlinear Behaviour}
SHAP analysis was applied to the Random Forest to interpret feature effects. Mean absolute SHAP values identify review length (0.0306), expert tier (0.0273), Travel\_Year\_c (0.0219), review delay (0.0203), and museums (0.0182) as the most influential predictors.The SHAP summary shows that longer reviews increase incongruence, expert reviewers contribute positively (less experienced negatively), and incongruence declines over time, consistent with the logit model. Review delay has a modest, context-dependent effect, aligning with its non-significance in bivariate analysis and weak logit impact, suggesting interaction-driven influence. SHAP complements the logit by revealing nonlinear, context-dependent effects.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Structured Incongruence and Conservative Rating Behaviour }
An incongruence rate of 18.6\% (\~1 in 5 reviews) indicates systematic mismatch rather than random noise. In Sri Lanka, religious sites may induce high ratings despite mixed sentiment, while museums require multidimensional evaluation not captured by a single score; experienced reviewers may also write positively but rate conservatively. This structured, context-dependent behaviour is further reflected in the Conservative Rater pattern, which combines positive text with moderate ratings. It accounts for 38.4\% of incongruent reviews and represents the dominant mechanism of mismatch. Its prevalence increases from 27.4\% (novice) to 40.4\% (expert), supporting a calibration effect where experienced reviewers apply stricter standards. Together, these findings show that incongruence is systematic rather than random. \textbf{Implication:} Star ratings are unreliable ground-truth labels, and models may misinterpret positive experiences as neutral, particularly for expert-generated content, leading to systematic bias.


\subsection{Location Type as a Structural Moderator of Label Reliability}
Location type is a key determinant of incongruence. Museum reviews show higher odds of mismatch (Adjusted OR = 2.386) relative to national parks, with similar effects for beaches and bodies of water; zoological gardens and waterfalls are also significant. This reflects evaluative complexity, as multidimensional experiences (e.g., museums) are harder to capture with a single rating, while religious sites may show higher mismatch due to social pressures. These results indicate that label reliability varies with context. \textbf{Implication:} Treating all reviews uniformly may introduce systematic bias, highlighting the need for context-aware sentiment models. 

\subsection{Complementary Roles of Linear and Nonlinear Models}
The multi-model framework demonstrates that linear and nonlinear approaches provide complementary layers of insight rather than competing explanations. The logistic regression baseline achieves a test AUC of 0.584, establishing a stable reference for linear discriminative capacity, while the explanatory logit model identifies 19 statistically significant predictors, providing interpretable effect sizes and clear directional relationships. The Random Forest model achieves a higher test AUC of 0.6095, confirming the presence of nonlinear relationships and interaction effects. However, the modest gain indicates nonlinearities are secondary drivers of incongruence. 

Importantly, the overall predictive performance (AUC $\approx 0.58$--$0.61$) should not be viewed as a limitation of model quality, but as evidence of the inherently complex and partially unobservable nature of the phenomenon. While statistically significant predictors exist, their moderate effect sizes suggest that the signal is distributed across multiple factors, with remaining variation driven by latent behavioural processes such as reviewer calibration and contextual norms. This establishes a key contribution: modest predictive performance itself is an informative finding, demonstrating that incongruence is structured yet difficult to predict. SHAP analysis reinforces this interpretation by revealing how features such as reviewer expertise, review length, and venue type contribute in nonlinear and context-dependent ways. Crucially, these insights do not contradict the logit model; instead, they show that nonlinear methods capture heterogeneity within effects that linear models necessarily average. This implies that improving predictive performance requires incorporating latent behavioural and contextual signals, and that star ratings are not fully reliable ground-truth labels due to systematic, context-dependent distortions. 

\subsection{Review Delay and Reviewer Expertise Effects }
Review delay shows no significant effect in bivariate analysis and only a weak negative association in the logit model (OR = 0.960). Although ranked as relatively influential by SHAP, its effect is modest and context-dependent, indicating it is a secondary factor rather than a primary driver of incongruence. In contrast, mismatch patterns vary systematically with reviewer expertise: Conservative Rater behaviour increases from 27.4\% (novice) to 40.4\% (expert), while extreme patterns such as Harsh Deflator decrease from 10.8\% to 4.2\%.This suggests experienced reviewers are more calibrated, with incongruence driven more by reviewer traits than time, underscoring the need for reviewer-level modelling.

 


\section{Conclusion}
This study shows that sentiment–rating incongruence in tourism reviews is not random noise but a structured phenomenon driven by reviewer behaviour and contextual evaluation complexity. Using statistical inference, predictive modelling, and interpretable machine learning, mismatch is linked to mechanisms such as reviewer calibration, multidimensional evaluation, and context-specific rating norms. Several limitations should be noted. The analysis is based on a single Sri Lankan dataset, limiting generalizability. It relies on structured features and sentiment labels, which may introduce measurement error and overlook deeper linguistic nuances. The modest predictive performance (AUC $\approx$ 0.58–0.61) suggests substantial unexplained variance, likely driven by latent behavioural and contextual factors, and the observational design limits causal inference. Despite this, the findings highlight a key insight: incongruence is structured yet only partially observable, revealing limitations in treating star ratings as reliable ground truth. This has important implications for NLP and platform design, as systematic label distortion may bias models and aggregated ratings may underrepresent true sentiment.Rating–text inconsistency is a meaningful behavioural signal, not noise; future work should extend this across contexts and incorporate deeper semantic features to improve explanatory power.
